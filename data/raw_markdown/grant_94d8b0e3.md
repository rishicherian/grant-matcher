Boston, MA – Massachusetts has allocated nearly $1.2 million through the Fisheries Innovation Fund to bolster the state’s commercial fishing industry, focusing on innovation, safety, and resilience in the face of expanding offshore wind development.
The six grants, announced this week, support projects ranging from gear modifications compatible with wind farms to marketing efforts and public education campaigns. Administered as a partnership between the state and the fishing sector, the fund aims to promote coexistence between fisheries and renewable energy infrastructure while enhancing profitability and community strength.
**Key projects include:**
* University of Massachusetts Dartmouth’s School for Marine Science and Technology (SMAST) receiving $265,984 to develop and test lighted fish pots for species like black sea bass and scup, allowing safe deployment within wind farm areas.
* Commercial Fisheries Research Foundation awarded two grants totaling over $322,000: one for evaluating mechanical squid jigging gear and another for testing bottom trawl configurations near wind farm cables to improve safety.
* New Bedford Port Authority granted $250,000 for promotional campaigns to boost visibility and consumer demand for seafood from the nation’s top fishing port.
* F/V Martha Elizabeth, a commercial fishing vessel, securing $174,908 to innovate traps for fish and sea scallops suited to offshore wind zones.
* Eating with the Ecosystem Inc. funded with $156,533 to produce events and videos highlighting local seafood caught near wind farms, involving fishers, chefs, and consumers.
“Massachusetts is home to a nation-leading seafood industry and thriving blue economy,” said Energy and Environmental Affairs Secretary Rebecca Tepper. ” Investing in the strength and resilience of our commercial fishing industry is crucial to sustain our local economies and coastal culture.
“Department of Fish and Game Commissioner Tom O’Shea emphasized the fund’s role in supporting those navigating offshore wind changes, benefiting fishers, for-hire vessels, and shoreside businesses alike.
The initiative includes roughly $375,000 in subcontracts to the industry, with significant portions going directly to commercial fishermen and involving multiple vessels in collaborative research.
As offshore wind projects advance in Massachusetts waters, these investments underscore the state’s commitment to ensuring its historic fishing communities adapt and thrive alongside clean energy goals.
