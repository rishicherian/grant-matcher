Seth Kaplan’s recent insights on tackling inequality in Atlanta really resonate with the approach we must take in urban development. When it comes to building durable, generational growth, making the neighborhood the focal point of transformation is absolutely the way to go.
Too often, revitalization efforts try to boil the ocean, spreading resources and attention so thin that the impact is ultimately diluted. However, hyper-focusing investment on a specific, targeted area creates a much stronger foundation for real, sustainable community change. Concentrating our efforts allows us to address the unique fabric of a neighborhood—its housing, local businesses, and community spaces—in a holistic and meaningful way.
I'm incredibly excited about neighborhood-first approaches here in Newark, and I look forward to putting that excitement in to action.

Cities that are vibrant, lasting, and equitable are made up of a collection of strong neighborhoods that emphasize people, place, and sustainability.
💡Quality of life is impacted most and measured best at the neighborhood level.
💡What one neighborhood needs is likely different from other neighborhoods in a city, and "return on taxpayer investment" and "quality of life" have different definitions for different people living in different locations and in varying context/conditions.
💡While a "one size fits all" approach might seem like the most efficient way to serve residents, solutions customized for each neighborhood will have a higher impact on QOL and perceived ROI for the residents living there.
💡Sometimes the smallest investments have the biggest impacts. Cities don't have to spend a ton of money to make residents happy.
💡Local citizens and businesses have time, talent, and/or treasure ($) to invest into their neighborhood. They usually just need guidance to know where it's best applied and have confidence from the city that their participation is wanted and allowed.
1️⃣ Get the big things right citywide, like vision, community identity, and guiding principles (e.g. fiscal and financial sustainability, diversified housing and neighborhoods, safe and accessible mobility options for everyone, etc). IF big ticket projects are on the table, make darn sure they will actually result in enhanced quality of life and generate sufficient revenue to pay back the investment. Otherwise, you may have a fancy ribbon cutting and short term sugar high, but end up with a liability that hammers your city's fiscal health for years if not decades.
2️⃣ Put incremental enhancement plans together to identify low cost, high ROI improvements neighborhood by neighborhood.
3️⃣ Collaborate w the entire community to identify, connect, align, and empower public, private, and philanthropic resources to make meaningful progress toward shared outcomes, right now, with resources you already have.
4️⃣ Implement, measure and iterate, then repeat.

An experiment in Atlanta led by Mayor Andre Dickens is showing “how the assumptions undergirding investments in welfare, public goods, and urban renewal can be reframed,” writes Seth Kaplan in SSIR’s new spring issue.
Based on Dickens’ contention that strong neighborhoods uplift struggling people, enhance the economy, and attract talent that will help the city thrive, the mayor is attempting to restructure government around neighborhoods to “achieve fairness of place.”
The mayor and his new 20-person team have targeted six of the city’s 40 neighborhoods, focusing on four areas to transform them into desired destinations: education, housing, commercial corridors, and the physical landscape.
Will the experiment shift philanthropy’s focus away from improving a single person’s needs to addressing the root and recurring causes of individuals’ problems that often tie back to the distress of the neighborhoods they call home?
Time will tell, Kaplan writes, but Atlanta’s past success with place-based project “shows what is possible and why it’s worthwhile to chart this course.”

Last week’s Neighborhood Reinvestment Initiative Commission meeting reinforced something simple, but critical:
Collaboration isn’t optional.
We’re all working to serve the same communities. But too often, we approach the work in silos, rather than in alignment.
The thing is, people don’t experience systems in silos. Housing, education, and public investment show up together in everyday life. It’s all connected. One example of this came up in our conversation around Tax Allocation Districts (TADs), a tool that helps fund community development and infrastructure.
There are different perspectives on whether TADs should be extended. From where I sit, they’re still one of the most effective tools we have for catalyzing equitable development, especially in neighborhoods that have historically faced underinvestment.
At FCS - Focused Community Strategies, we’ve seen this firsthand:
The Beltline TAD helped make our Brownsville Pointe development possible, bringing affordable housing to Historic South Atlanta in a way that otherwise may not have happened. And because of the Beltline TAD, there is more affordable housing to come in Historic South Atlanta. These tools aren’t perfect. But when they’re used well, they open real pathways for stability and opportunity.
If we’re serious about building a stronger Atlanta for all, we’ve got to move beyond silos and toward shared outcomes. Because at the end of the day, this work isn’t about any one entity. It’s about the people we’re all here to serve.
Would love to hear how others are thinking about this: what does real collaboration look like in your work?

Why do some cities grow—and still feel stuck?
Most conversations about inequality focus on people: income, education, or jobs. But what if one of the most consequential forms of inequality is between neighborhoods within the same city?
I’ve been working on a framework called the Neighborhood Inequality Index (NII) that tries to measure exactly that.
Instead of looking at citywide averages, the index asks a different question:
How far apart are the neighborhoods inside a metro area?
The idea is simple: when opportunity, investment, and social networks are highly uneven across neighborhoods, it doesn’t just affect residents. It can also shape a city’s economic trajectory, fiscal health, and ability to attract talent and investment.
The index combines four types of neighborhood dispersion:
• Mobility dispersion – how much adult outcomes for low-income children vary across neighborhoods
• Poverty concentration – how strongly disadvantage clusters geographically
• Income & wealth dispersion – differences in neighborhood income and housing values
• Social capital dispersion – variation in cross-class social connectedness
When these gaps are large, cities often experience higher public costs, underutilized talent, and fragmented growth patterns.
The table below shows an illustrative ranking of the top 25 U.S. metros by neighborhood-based inequality.
The goal isn’t to name and shame cities—it’s to ask a harder question:
Should urban success be measured partly by whether neighborhoods are converging rather than diverging?
I’m very interested in feedback from people working in urban policy, economic development, housing, and local government.
Are these the right metrics to measure given the goal?
What would your city look like through this lens?
And should narrowing neighborhood gaps be a core measure of urban performance?
