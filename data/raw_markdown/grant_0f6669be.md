This is EXACTLY what we mean, when we recommend "leaning into local!
Bethlehem, PA — the former rust-belt, steel-town-that-could, commonly known as "Christmas City, USA" is a marvelous example of 21st century reinvention. And I know this for a fact because I lived there briefly in my late teens when, frankly, it wasn't so hot. The limping city, anchored by prestigious Lehigh University was still reeling from the post-industrial closure of its primary industry; The Bethlehem Steel Works. Since then, the decaying, ruin-porn, former brownfield site has been converted into a multi-use venue known as "Steel Stacks" that draws big name artists, a revered multi-day cultural festival and more. But I digress..
The point here being that Bethlehem, a modest city on the upswing, with a current population hovering around 80K and growing — and a solid hour and a half drive from either Philly or NYC — is leaning into local for America 250. Yes, the once-powerful city that built a plurality of America's mighty infrastructure with its eponymous steel production during the industrial revolution and well into the 20th century — today this quaint and historic hamlet wields massive soft power. Bethlehem is culturally significant to the birth of the nation, and rightly so, should be featured in this year's semiquincentennial celebrations (like just about every single place in the US today, tbh). With an enviable driveshed tourism population, many of whom may be looking to escape the fandom and brouhaha in their own host cities this summer, Bethlehem stands to win big.
Its proximity to major East Coast metros, stretching from Norfolk and Richmond, VA to DC, Baltimore, MD, Wilmington, DE on up through Connecticut, Rhode Island, and even Boston, Mass and Pittsburgh, PA (another former rust-belt, ruin-porn white paper on recovery and resilience — but that's a note for another time), renders it an admirable offset to the heat, crowds and congestion of nearby FIFA host cities, celebrations and grueling summer traffic.
The city has long been a local PA destination at the holidays, and has leaned into its municipal moniker as an essential stanchion of its civic brand — and with good reason. Across "Pennsy Dutch country", and around the state and region, many a holiday home is decorated with the quintessential "Moravian Star" — emblematic and culturally derivative of the city's founding heritage. Cobblestone streets and alluring shops line its main business corridor, enchanting visitors by the thousands every Christmas and New Years.
But this year, Bethlehemites are poised to reap year-round benefit from the country's 250th Anniversary — by doing EXACTLY what we've been recommending to cities small and large for years now. They're leaning into local. And this is a beautiful example of just how they're doing it:

We get it. It can be challenging for place management practitioners to see their districts through fresh eyes. The morning work ritual; seeing the same civic infrastructure; stoplights and crosswalks with pedestrian commuters, Block by Block employees power-washing graffiti, vomit and pet waste off the streets, the same welcoming barista at your fave java joint dispensing the familiar-yet-somewhat-muted smile along with your morning fix, the angry voicemails from ratepayers complaining about the issue — it all detracts from seeing your city as a new visitor might. We tend to overlook the everyday when it seems static. You've seen your city's hidden gems and points of pride hundreds of times. It's not new to you, yet collectively, these little indicators make or break the sense of place a newbie might experience.
A few years back, then-Board Chair
Kate Borders
of the
International Downtown Association
wrote a compelling piece in the IDA newsletter about seeing your district through fresh eyes. And that singular piece has stuck in our craw for years! Because it held truths and clarity about how we do our work. And when we become blind and immune to the potency of soft power, it often alludes us. And that's not good news for those of us tasked with storytelling a district's attractions, merits and must-see assets.
That's why we included "leaning into local" as one of our Top Three Strategic Pivots you need to make to stay afloat — considering the tourism turmoil and general worldwide standing of our country in this essential, semiquincentennial year. The irony is not lost on us. As a nation, indeed we should be celebrating the 250th anniversary of our democracy. And every hamlet, vale and unincorporated municipality in America has the opportunity to join in these celebrations — and to wield incomparable soft power as a destination. You have the assets, historical sites, plus bars, restaurants and shops to make your place attractive to "somebody" this summer. Take a page outta Bethlehem's playbook, and lean into local.
Not sure how to do it? We've published a telling Trends Report intended for all cities, but mainly geared to the smaller underdogs in the nation. We can't all have a Liberty Bell, Brooklyn Bridge or St. Louis Arch drawing tourists to our destinations this summer. But we can all take stock in what we do have; our assets, our history and most of all — our communities.
