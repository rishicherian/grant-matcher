Scranton eyes $3M in grants for improvements downtown and in Pine Brook
=======================================================================
Projects propose bicycle lane, building renovations on Lackawanna Avenue; new sidewalks and Sweeney's Beach connector in Pine Brook

SCRANTON — Officials plan to seek $3 million in state grants for streetscape and neighborhood improvements, including creating a bicycle lane on Lackawanna Avenue downtown and connecting a park in Pine Brook to neighboring Sweeney’s Beach along the Lackawanna River.
Here’s a look at each project.
#### Lackawanna Avenue pedestrian and bike improvements
A $1.26 million multimodal grant from the state Department of Transportation for pedestrian and bicycle infrastructure improvements along approximately 1,000 linear feet of the south side of Lackawanna Avenue, between Adams and Penn avenues, and which are the 300, 400 and 500 blocks of Lackawanna Avenue. The work would include “construction of a sidewalk-level, protected bike lane with a stamped concrete buffer,” as well as replacement of deteriorated sidewalks and curbs with disabled-accessible ramps to enhance accessibility, improvement of crosswalks and upgrades of stormwater management infrastructure. Concrete medians of varying widths and lengths in the 500 block of Lackawanna Avenue also would be removed to make more room for street parking in that block, city Business Administrator Eileen Cipriani said.
“The proposed transportation improvements are needed to address significant safety, accessibility, and connectivity deficiencies along Lackawanna Avenue, a heavily-traveled corridor in downtown Scranton,” the resolution says. “The existing infrastructure is outdated, with deteriorating sidewalks, limited ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) accessibility and no dedicated bicycle facilities — creating unsafe conditions for pedestrians and cyclists.”
The project flows from a 2023 connectivity study, which had also been called a walkability study, and that proposed numerous ways to improve pedestrian safety and traffic flow throughout the downtown.

#### 514, 516 and 518 Lackawanna Ave.
A $1 million grant from the state Department of Community and Economic Development’s “Main Street Matters” program to assist a project by Frank Dubas’ Slocum Hollow Properties LLC in the 500 block of Lackawanna Avenue. Dubas plans to renovate three vacant contiguous buildings he owns at 514, 516 and 518 Lackawanna Ave. into commercial and residential spaces. The three buildings with approximately 30,000 square feet of space are listed on the National Register of Historic Places and qualify for federal and state historic tax credits.
The $1 million Main Street Matters grant would complete a $7.5 million project estimate. The other funding sources include $2.8 million in private developer’s capital; a $2 million grant from the state Redevelopment Assistance Capital Program (RACP); a $1.5 million loan from the Pennsylvania Housing Finance Ageny’s ReCLAIM (Revised Community Leveraging Assistance Initiative Mortgage) program; and $200,000 in state historic tax credits. Dubas hopes to develop the three buildings for residential and commercial occupancy by mid-2026.

#### Capouse sidewalk improvement
A $500,000 DCED multimodal grant to improve sidewalks on Penn and Capouse avenues in Pine Brook, between Vine and East Gibson streets. In this stretch, existing sidewalks and curbing in poor condition pose safety hazards for pedestrians, particularly those with disabilities, as many crossings lack ADA-compliant ramps and proper pavement markings, according to pending legislation.
“The corridor serves as a critical connection for residents, businesses and schools, and the improvements are essential to enhance safety, support multimodal transportation and promote economic revitalization through a more-accessible and attractive streetscape,” legislation says.
This streetscape project under development with the United Neighborhood Centers of Northeastern Pennsylvania, which leads Pine Brook revitalization efforts, represents a key component of improving walkability in this blighted gateway of the Pine Brook neighborhood. A five-year revitalization plan issued in April 2021 placed a focus on improving “catalyst” areas, including the Capouse Avenue gateway at Olive Street.
This project would focus on enhancing pedestrian safety, accessibility and connectivity along Penn and Capouse avenues, by replacing sidewalks, installing ADA-compliant crossings and clearly marked pedestrian routes, and traffic-calming measures such as stamped crosswalks and curbing. “These improvements will serve residents, businesses, and visitors alike, boosting safety, encouraging foot traffic, supporting local commerce and fostering long-term neighborhood vitality,” the resolution says. “The project will improve mobility for all users, reduce pedestrian-vehicle conflicts and create a safer, more walkable environment that supports multimodal transportation and revitalizes the streetscape.”

#### Chic Feldman Field to Sweeney’s Beach connector
A $333,576 state DCED Main Street Matters grant to construct stairs to connect the rear of Chic Feldman Field to the adjacent Sweeney’s Beach area. The field sits atop a steep slope along the Sweeney’s Beach road and parking area. This project, done in collaboration with UNC, would build an 8-foot-wide reinforced concrete stairway approximately 60 feet long with a 19-foot elevation gain. The stairway would feature an intermediate landing for rest and safety, along with railings on both sides to ensure compliance and support. Additionally, a 10-foot-wide bituminous trail would be installed, extending 50 feet from the Sweeney’s Beach parking area to the base of the stairway, and 250 feet from the top of the stairway to the Chic Feldman Field parking area. Lighting fixtures would be installed along the entire pathway to enhance visibility and promote safety.
“By addressing the elevation barrier and integrating thoughtful infrastructure, the project will foster greater connectivity between the two parks and encourage more frequent and inclusive community use,” a resolution says.
Sierra Radocesky of West Scranton grew up in Pine Brook and visits her father there daily and they frequently visit Chic Feldman Field for fresh air, sunshine and exercise. She was not aware of the city plans to connect to the rear of the Feldman park with Sweeney’s Beach down a steep, forested slope, but said it sounds like a good idea.
“That would be great. I didn’t even know this was back here,” she said of the adjacent Sweeney’s Beach obscured from view from Chic Feldman Field by trees and thick vegetation.

At its 6:30 p.m. meeting Tuesday at City Hall, Scranton City Council will vote on introducing resolutions from the administration of Mayor Paige Gebhardt Cognetti authorizing the city to seek the grants for the Dubas project and Sweeney/Feldman connector. Council also is scheduled to vote on adopting the other two resolutions introduced last week.
