Yesterday I sat in a room full of people who believe, deeply, evidentially, practically that what happens in the earliest years of a child’s life shapes everything that follows.
As someone who works in this space every day, and as a parent navigating that journey firsthand, it was one of those rare days that felt both validating and galvanising.
The Brighter Futures Jersey conference on early intervention brought together researchers, policymakers, charities, clinicians and practitioners, all working from different angles, but pulled by exactly the same conviction.
And that, in itself, was powerful.
As a parent of a child with significant additional needs, I’ve lived the difference that early intervention makes, not as a theory, but as a daily reality. It’s part of why this work feels so personal, and so urgent.
The conversation covered a lot of ground. The economic case for early investment. How to turn data into insight, insight into programmes, and programmes into policy. The human cost, not just the financial one, of failing to intervene early. Solution-focused approaches that build confidence and self-efficacy in families. The difference between knowing what works and actually making it happen at a systems level.
But the thread running through all of it was the same:
Early intervention is not a nice-to-have.
It is not soft.
It is not fluffy.
It is the smartest, most evidence-backed investment we can make in children, families, and society.
What strikes me most, both in this work and in building Parent Sense, is how often early childhood support gets filed under “good to have.” A warm add-on rather than a driver of real outcomes.
The research says otherwise.
The human cost of not intervening says otherwise.
We spend enormous resources addressing problems at the point of greatest harm, in schools, in healthcare systems, in social services, when the evidence consistently shows that earlier is better, cheaper, and more effective.
The question isn’t whether we can afford to invest in early years.
It’s whether we can afford not to.
At Parent Sense, this is the belief our entire platform is built on. Empowering parents in the earliest years isn’t a nice gesture. It’s one of the highest-leverage interventions available.
Days like yesterday remind me why this work matters, and why the people committed to it need to keep making the case, loudly and with evidence, until it becomes impossible to ignore.
Thank you to Brighter Futures for creating a space for exactly that conversation, and for bringing together individuals and organisations so incredibly passionate about this space.

Why Early Childhood Education is One of the Most Powerful Investments We Can Make
At Project Hope, we are often asked a simple question: Why early childhood education?
The answer is both deeply human and strongly supported by evidence.
Research consistently shows that the highest return on investment in education happens in the earliest years of a child’s life. In fact, studies by leading economist James Heckman indicate that every R1 invested in quality early childhood development can yield returns of up to R7–R13 over time.
But what does this really mean?
It means that when a child has access to early learning, nutrition, and emotional support:
• They are more likely to succeed in school
• They are more likely to earn a stable income as adults
• They are less likely to rely on social support systems
• They are more likely to raise healthier, more secure families of their own
In short, early investment doesn’t just change one life - it shifts entire generational trajectories.
In communities where poverty is deeply entrenched, the gap begins early. By the age of five, children from disadvantaged backgrounds can already be significantly behind in language, cognitive development, and social-emotional skills.
Without intervention, this gap often widens over time.
At Project Hope, our work is rooted in closing that gap - not just through education, but through a holistic model that supports the child, the caregiver, and the broader environment in which they are growing.
Because breaking the cycle of poverty is not about a single moment of change. It is about consistent, intentional investment in the earliest years, when it matters most.
This is the first of a series where we will share more about how we do this work, and what we are learning along the way.

PEAK Grantmaking
Imagine opening a community center. You put your money, time, and resources into this to make a difference. You begin a literacy program, but the kids aren't listening. They come but they're loud. Would you keep going? Write them off? Or take the time to find out why they're loud. Jackie Joyner took the time. And it led to realizing the kids were hungry. Well before "hangry" became a term, she experienced what happens to learning when children are undernourished. The seemingly simple act of adding a meal to the program delivery increased engagement, retained staff, and sustained a community center that has now crossed the 26-year mark. Jackie Joyner-Kersee (JJK) Community Center, located at 101 Jackie Joyner Kersee Circle in East St. Louis, IL, is a pillar of the community.
#PEAK2026STL opened with a keynote that was more than a keynote. It was an inside look into what can happen when experience, need, and resources are combined with values. Olympian Jackie Joyner-Kersee had a life that was both extraordinary and ordinary. Ordinary in the sense that her challenges and circumstances are not unlike those of the people who live in her community. Extraordinary in that she was well loved, talented, and determined to the point of stubbornness. What started as a question she needed answered for herself during a difficult time, "where do the children go when they need to be lifted up?" has grown into a 41K sq-ft facility serving more than 10K families annually.
Ms. Kersee's journey highlighted lessons for those in attendance, whether you are a funder, recipient, or steward of community.
1️⃣ Be flexible in how you fulfill your mission. Had she and her team not been open to learning in real-time, the addition of food might have been missed, and wrong misconceptions and assumptions would have been placed on the very children they wanted to serve. So whether it is your funding rollout, your community program, or what you think is helpful, be flexible and always keep learning. Pay attention.
2️⃣ If you believe in your mission, you will find a way to get it done. The proof is in the work. The JJK Community Center now serves 300–400 children daily. That does not happen without a team that refused to give up on the mission even when it was hard.
3️⃣"Take a trip and see the work." A scheduled site visit is inherently curated. The recipient wants to show their best, and there is nothing wrong with that. But if you truly want to understand the pulse of a community, go as a person. Not as a funder with a clipboard. Walk in, look around, and pay attention to what the needs actually are, not just what is being presented to you.
4️⃣ "Don't only invest in the community, you need to have a vested interest in the community." There is a difference. One is a transaction. The other is a relationship.
